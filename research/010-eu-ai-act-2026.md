# 010 — EU AI Act Article 50 — 2 August 2026 application details

## Question
What does EU AI Act Article 50 require of Paradigm specifically when its transparency obligations become applicable on 2 August 2026, and what does the spec need to do to comply on day one?

## Why it matters (blast radius)
Paradigm targets a 2026 ship. Article 50 of the EU AI Act becomes applicable on 2 August 2026 [1, 2] and imposes transparency obligations on providers of generative AI systems whose outputs include synthetic image, audio, video, or text. Non-compliance is enforceable. The spec already includes C2PA, image and audio watermarks, and a content hash — but the spec needs to be checked against the *actual text* of Article 50 plus the European Commission's draft Code of Practice [3, 4, 5] to confirm the existing primitives discharge the legal obligations. This brief gates `compliance/eu-ai-act.md`.

## What we know from the spec
- `compliance/eu-ai-act.md` exists as a placeholder noting that the spec must comply with Article 50.
- The spec already includes C2PA manifests, DCT image watermarks, and audiowmark audio watermarks.
- The spec already includes a content hash and ECDSA signatures.

## Findings

1. **Article 50 of the EU AI Act becomes fully applicable on 2 August 2026,** 24 months after the Act's entry into force. [1, 2] This is a hard date — Paradigm must be compliant on day one for any user in the EU.

2. **The core obligation for generative AI providers** (Article 50(2), as summarized in [1, 6]) is: "Providers of AI systems, including general-purpose AI systems, generating synthetic audio, image, video or text content, shall ensure that the outputs of the AI system are marked in a machine-readable format and detectable as artificially generated or manipulated." Two requirements: **machine-readable marking** and **detectability**.

3. **The marking must be (a) effective, (b) interoperable, (c) robust, and (d) reliable as far as is technically feasible,** taking into account the specificities and limitations of the various types of content, the costs of implementation, and the generally acknowledged state of the art. [6] This is the "state of the art" defense — providers do not have to use technology that does not exist, but they do have to use what does.

4. **Deepfake disclosure (Article 50(4))** requires deployers of AI systems that generate or manipulate image, audio, or video content constituting a deepfake to disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated. There are art/satire exceptions but they are narrow.

5. **The European Commission published the first draft of the Code of Practice on Marking and Labelling of AI-Generated Content on 17 December 2025.** [3, 4, 5] A second draft was expected in March 2026 and a final code in June 2026, ahead of Article 50's August 2026 application date. The Code of Practice is voluntary but creates a presumption of compliance for providers who follow it.

6. **The Code of Practice draft endorses C2PA as a primary mechanism for the machine-readable marking obligation,** alongside watermarking. [3, 5, 7] This is the most important finding for Paradigm: the spec's existing C2PA + watermark architecture is *aligned with the regulatory direction*, not at odds with it.

7. **The Code of Practice draft also calls for watermarking that survives common transformations** (recompression, format conversion, screenshotting) as a complement to C2PA, since C2PA metadata can be stripped. This is exactly the reasoning the Paradigm spec already follows in `compliance/watermark-image.md` and `compliance/watermark-audio.md`.

8. **Text content marking is harder** [3, 5] and the Code of Practice acknowledges this. The state of the art for text watermarking is much weaker than for images/audio, and providers are expected to use the best feasible mechanism (e.g., post-hoc detector models, statistical watermarks like Aaronson's scheme) without strict bit-recovery guarantees. **Paradigm currently does not generate plain text outputs** — the GSPL DSL is the input language, not a text output. This narrows the scope considerably.

9. **The "deepfake" definition in Article 3(60)** covers AI-generated or AI-manipulated image, audio, or video content that appreciably resembles existing persons, objects, places, or events and would falsely appear to a person to be authentic. This is a triggering condition for the disclosure obligation (Article 50(4)), not just the marking obligation (Article 50(2)).

10. **Penalties** (Article 99) for non-compliance with Article 50 obligations are up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher), per the Act's penalty framework. This is the second tier of penalties (the highest tier is €35M / 7% for prohibited practices).

## Risks identified

- **The Code of Practice is still in draft.** The final version may add specific technical requirements (e.g., "the watermark MUST encode a specific OID," "the C2PA manifest MUST contain a specific assertion") that Paradigm must adopt at short notice between June 2026 (final Code) and August 2026 (application date) — a six-week window.
- **"Deepfake disclosure" is on the deployer, not the provider.** Paradigm is a provider (it generates synthetic content via the kernel pipeline). Whether a downstream user counts as a "deployer" under the Act depends on context. The spec should make it easy for downstream tools to surface the deepfake disclosure but should not assume the spec itself fulfills it.
- **The "state of the art" defense erodes over time.** A 2026-acceptable watermark may be inadequate by 2028 if better techniques become standard.
- **EU enforcement readiness** is uncertain. Penalties exist but enforcement infrastructure is still being stood up. The spec should comply on day one regardless.
- **Non-EU jurisdictions** (US California SB 942, UK, China) have parallel and overlapping obligations. The spec should design for the union, not the EU minimum.

## Recommendation

**The existing Paradigm primitives (C2PA + image watermark + audio watermark + content hash + signature) already cover the *technical* requirements of Article 50(2) for image, audio, and video modalities. The brief recommends:**

1. **Treat the existing C2PA + watermark architecture as load-bearing for EU AI Act compliance** and document it as such in `compliance/eu-ai-act.md`. Do not add new technical primitives just for the AI Act unless the final Code of Practice (June 2026) demands them.
2. **Track the Code of Practice drafts** (March 2026 second draft, June 2026 final) and reserve a one-week spec update window in July 2026 to fold in any specific requirements.
3. **Add a Paradigm-specific C2PA assertion** (custom assertion label, e.g., `com.paradigm.gspl-seed`) that includes the canonical seed hash. This makes Paradigm-generated content trivially machine-detectable beyond the generic C2PA `c2pa.actions` assertion.
4. **Document explicitly what Paradigm does NOT cover under Article 50:**
   - Text generation (Paradigm has no text output modality at v1).
   - The deployer-side deepfake disclosure obligation under Article 50(4) (this is the responsibility of any product that uses Paradigm to generate content depicting real persons).
5. **Add a `compliance/eu-ai-act-mapping.md` document** that maps each Article 50 obligation to the Paradigm primitive that fulfills it, with citations. This is the document a regulator would ask for, and writing it now forces honesty about coverage.
6. **Stand on the "state of the art" defense for the watermark robustness limitations from Briefs 008 and 009.** Document the robustness envelope honestly; the Act does not require infallible marking, only state-of-the-art marking.
7. **Reserve the right to mark Paradigm exports with multiple assertions** (Paradigm-specific + a generic "AI-generated" tag) so generic AI-content detectors can pick them up without needing to know about Paradigm specifically.
8. **Mirror the same compliance posture for California SB 942** in a parallel document (`compliance/ca-sb942.md`). The technical requirements are very similar and the same primitives discharge both.

## Confidence
**4/5.** Article 50's text is final law. The Code of Practice drafts are public and consistent. The spec's existing architecture is already aligned with the regulatory direction. The 4/5 reflects the residual risk that the *final* Code of Practice (June 2026) will add specific technical requirements not visible in the December 2025 draft.
