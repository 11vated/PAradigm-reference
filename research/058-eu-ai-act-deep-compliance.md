# 058 — EU AI Act deep compliance

## Question
Beyond the high-level pass in Brief 010, what is the *operational* compliance posture for the EU AI Act across GSPL's substrate, agent, marketplace, and shipped models — and what risk classification does GSPL fall under?

## Why it matters
The AI Act is in force. Non-compliance is a fine of up to 7% of global revenue or €35M, whichever is higher. The Act treats different AI uses differently: some are banned, some are high-risk and heavily regulated, some are minimal-risk with only transparency obligations. GSPL must know exactly where it stands and what it must do.

## What we know from the spec
- Brief 010: high-level AI Act compliance pass.
- Brief 046: model cards for shipped models.
- Brief 056: telemetry and privacy.

## Findings — risk classification

### GSPL is *not* prohibited (Article 5)
None of the prohibited AI practices apply:
- No social scoring.
- No real-time biometric identification.
- No emotion recognition in workplaces or schools.
- No exploitation of vulnerable groups.
- No subliminal manipulation.

### GSPL is *not* high-risk (Annex III)
The high-risk categories cover:
- Biometric ID, critical infrastructure, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, migration, justice.

GSPL is a creative tool. None of these apply.

### GSPL is in the *limited risk / transparency* tier (Article 50)
This tier has *transparency obligations* for:
- AI systems that interact with humans (chatbots).
- AI systems that generate or manipulate content (image, audio, video generation).
- AI systems that recognize emotions or biometric categorization.

GSPL falls into the second category clearly — it generates content. The obligations:

#### Article 50(2): Mark generated content
Output of AI systems must be marked as artificially generated or manipulated, in a machine-readable format, where technically feasible.
- **Compliance:** every GSPL-generated output carries a c2pa-rs attestation (Brief 008) declaring it was generated by GSPL with the seed lineage. Watermarks (Brief 009) provide a forensic backstop.

#### Article 50(3): Inform users they're interacting with AI
Users interacting with AI systems must be informed they're interacting with AI, unless this is obvious.
- **Compliance:** the studio is *obviously* an AI tool — the chat interface, the agent, the variant grids all make this clear. The studio has a persistent "AI-assisted" badge in the title bar.

#### Article 50(4): Deepfake disclosure
Deepfakes (AI-generated content that resembles real persons, places, events) must be disclosed.
- **Compliance:** GSPL doesn't natively generate likenesses of real people, but users *could* via custom seeds. The c2pa attestation always declares AI origin. The studio surfaces a warning when generating likeness-like content; explicit deepfake mode requires checkbox acknowledgment.

### Article 53: General-purpose AI models
GSPL ships LLM models for the agent (Brief 055). Are these covered by Article 53?
- **Models with >10^25 FLOPs of training compute** are general-purpose AI models with systemic risk. None of GSPL's shipped models hit this.
- **Models below the threshold** still have general-purpose AI obligations: training data summary, copyright compliance, EU-market distribution requirements.

GSPL ships model cards (Brief 046) that meet the training data summary requirements. Models are downloaded from upstream providers (Meta, Mistral, Qwen, etc.) who are responsible for their own training compliance.

### Article 53(2): Open-source exemption
Open-weight AI models with publicly available parameters and weights are exempt from some obligations *unless* they have systemic risk.
- **GSPL itself is open source**, which qualifies for the open-source exemption on the operational obligations.
- **The shipped models** are all open weight; their providers handle the high-level obligations.

## Geographic considerations

The AI Act applies to:
- AI systems placed on the EU market.
- AI systems used in the EU.
- AI systems whose output is used in the EU.

GSPL is global; some users will be in the EU. Compliance is the floor, not regional.

## Risks identified

- **Article 50 interpretation drift:** the European Commission may issue guidance that changes what "marking" means. Mitigation: stay current with guidance; the studio's marking implementation is configurable.
- **High-risk reclassification:** if GSPL adds features that touch high-risk categories (e.g., emotion recognition for critic ensembles), the classification changes. Mitigation: deliberate scope; legal review before adding any potentially high-risk feature.
- **Shipped model provider compliance:** if Meta or Mistral fall out of compliance, GSPL ships their non-compliant model. Mitigation: model registry tracks compliance status; non-compliant models are removed.
- **Watermark tampering:** users strip watermarks. Mitigation: c2pa attestations are tamper-evident; the marking obligation is satisfied at generation time.
- **Cross-border enforcement:** different EU member states enforce differently. Mitigation: comply with the strictest interpretation.
- **Documentation drift:** the studio changes faster than the docs. Mitigation: docs updated per release; release blocks on doc parity.
