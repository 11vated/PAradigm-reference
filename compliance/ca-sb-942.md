# California SB 942 Compliance

## Scope

California Senate Bill 942, the **California AI Transparency Act**, was signed into law in September 2024 and applies from **1 January 2026**. It targets "covered providers" of generative AI systems with more than 1,000,000 monthly users in California, and it imposes two principal obligations:

1. **Disclosure tool** — provide a free public tool that allows users to determine whether image, audio, or video content was generated or significantly altered by the provider's GenAI system.
2. **Content provenance** — embed both **manifest provenance metadata** (machine-readable) and a **latent disclosure** (perceptual or technical, harder to remove) into every piece of GenAI content.

Paradigm is below the 1M Californian user threshold for the MVP and early-growth phases, and SB 942 therefore does not yet legally bind Paradigm. However, by our compliance principle of "conservative by default and globally," Paradigm implements SB 942 from day one. This avoids the need for a disruptive rollout if and when the threshold is crossed.

## Disclaimer

This document describes the engineering compliance approach. It is not legal advice. Paradigm Inc. retains California counsel for the formal compliance posture.

## SB 942 obligations in detail

### § 22757.1 — Disclosure tool

A covered provider must "make available an AI detection tool at no cost" that:

- Allows users to upload content and learn whether it was generated by the provider's system.
- Returns whether manifest provenance metadata exists and what it contains.
- Returns whether the latent disclosure is detectable.
- Does not require an account, payment, or registration.
- Does not retain uploaded content beyond what is necessary to perform the check.

**Paradigm's response:** the public verifier at `paradigm.app/verify` accepts uploads up to 50 MB (image, audio, video, `.gseed`), parses any C2PA manifest present, runs the latent watermark detector, and displays the result. Uploaded files are processed in-memory and discarded immediately; the only thing logged is the binary "match / no match" result for aggregated metrics. No account is required.

The tool is also available as a CLI: `npx @paradigm/verify <file>`.

### § 22757.2 — Content provenance disclosure

Every piece of GenAI image, audio, or video content produced by a covered provider must contain:

(a) A **manifest disclosure** consisting of at minimum:
- Name of the provider
- Name and version number of the GenAI system used
- Time and date of content creation/alteration
- A unique identifier
(b) A **latent disclosure** that is "detectable by an AI detection tool offered by the provider" and is "to the extent technically feasible, designed to be permanent or extraordinarily difficult to remove."

**Paradigm's manifest disclosure** is the C2PA manifest already specified in `compliance/c2pa.md`. It carries the provider name (`Paradigm Inc.`), the GSPL Agent version, the model used, the timestamp, and a unique seed hash. This trivially satisfies (a).

**Paradigm's latent disclosure** is implemented as follows, by content type:

| Content type | Latent disclosure |
|---|---|
| Image (PNG/JPEG/WebP) | DCT-domain watermark via the `imatag-py` library (port to Rust in `engine/watermark`). Embeds a 64-bit payload (truncated SHA-256 of seed hash) at PSNR ~46 dB so it is imperceptible. Recovery rate >95% after JPEG-90 reencoding, >80% after 0.5x downscale. |
| Audio (Opus/WAV) | Spread-spectrum watermark in the 2-8 kHz band via a port of `audiowmark`. Embeds a 64-bit payload. Recovery rate >90% after Opus-128k reencoding. |
| Video (MP4) | Same image watermark applied to every keyframe; redundancy gives high recovery even after re-encoding. |

The latent disclosure payload is the same 64-bit truncated seed hash; the verifier returns the recovered payload alongside the manifest.

### § 22757.3 — Removal and modification

Covered providers must "make a good faith effort" to ensure that downstream systems do not inadvertently strip the manifest disclosure. They must also disclose to licensees that altering the disclosure violates the act.

Paradigm's posture:
- Exports embed the manifest in format-native containers (not as a sidecar), which is the most resistant to incidental stripping.
- The Studio displays a banner the first time a user exports content that explains the content credentials and that stripping them is illegal in California.
- The terms of service for the Paradigm API require licensees not to strip credentials.

### § 22757.4 — Third-party licenses

Anyone licensing the GSPL Agent for white-label use must also embed the disclosures. The license terms enforce this contractually, and the API gateway *forces* the manifest into every export — there is no flag to disable it. This eliminates the practical possibility that a licensee could non-comply.

## Penalties

SB 942 carries civil penalties of up to **$5,000 per violation per day**, enforceable by the California Attorney General and city/county attorneys. Each unmarked piece of content is a separate violation. At Paradigm scale (1M users projected by end of 2027), even brief non-compliance could be financially fatal. The "compliance by construction" approach is therefore not optional engineering polish — it is a survival requirement.

## Verifier implementation

The public verifier is a small Fastify endpoint:

```
POST /verify
Content-Type: multipart/form-data
file: <binary>
```

Returns:

```json
{
  "manifest": {
    "present": true,
    "provider": "Paradigm Inc.",
    "system": "Paradigm GSPL Engine",
    "version": "1.0.0",
    "created_at": "2026-04-06T12:34:56Z",
    "seed_hash": "sha256:abc...",
    "valid_signature": true
  },
  "latent": {
    "present": true,
    "payload_hex": "abc1234567890def",
    "matches_manifest": true
  },
  "verdict": "Generated by Paradigm GSPL Engine",
  "confidence": 0.99
}
```

A `matches_manifest` of `true` is the strongest signal: it means the manifest and the latent watermark agree on the same seed hash, which is essentially impossible to forge without access to Paradigm's signing keys.

## Watermark robustness budget

Targets we test against on every release:

| Transformation | Min recovery rate |
|---|---|
| JPEG quality 90 | 95% |
| JPEG quality 70 | 80% |
| WebP quality 80 | 90% |
| 0.75× downscale | 90% |
| 0.50× downscale | 75% |
| Random crop 90% area | 85% |
| Gaussian noise σ=2 | 90% |
| Color quantization 8-bit | 85% |
| Screen-cap (full window) | 60% |
| Single-pass AI upscaler | 50% |

These are good but not perfect. SB 942 only requires "extraordinarily difficult to remove," not impossible, and the manifest disclosure is the primary signal. The latent disclosure exists to catch the case where someone strips the manifest deliberately.

## Operational integration

- The watermarking step runs in the export worker, between rendering and C2PA embedding.
- Watermark verification runs in the public verifier and also in a nightly sample of internal exports for QA.
- The watermark library version is pinned and tested against the robustness suite on every dependency bump.

## Open issues

1. **Audio watermarking on mono Opus** has lower recovery (~80%) than stereo. We plan to publish stereo by default for music exports.
2. **Lossless PNG re-encoding** preserves the watermark fully; lossy WebP at q<70 starts to degrade it. The Studio default export is PNG to avoid this.
3. **Cross-system attribution.** SB 942 requires that the verifier identify *Paradigm's* content, not that it identify *all* AI content. We do not need to detect Sora/MidJourney/etc., though we may add this as a courtesy.
